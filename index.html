For every non-trivial 4-digit number in base 10, how many applications of the routine does it take to reach the constant? The tallest bar — the mode, called out at top-right — is the most common answer, and it shifts with the base and digit count. Hanover's surprising result was specifically the 3-digit case: there the mode is 3 for every base above 4, which the overlay below reproduces.

Every number flows toward the constant. Columns are grouped by distance from the constant (leftmost = farthest); edge thickness shows how many integers travel each path.

The 3-digit constant in any even base

Hanover derives a closed form: for an even base b, the unique 3-digit Kaprekar constant is

( b/2 − 1 )( b − 1 )( b/2 )

Plug in b = 10 and you get 4 · 9 · 5 = 495. Odd bases have no 3-digit constant.

Why K(n) is always divisible by b − 1

Each digit pair contributes a factor of the form bx − by = by(b − 1)(1 + b + … ), so the whole difference carries a factor of b − 1. The output of the routine is always a multiple of the base minus one.

3-digit recurrence

For any 3-digit number with largest−smallest digit gap D, one step gives ( D−1 )( b−1 )( b−D ). Each further step shrinks the outer-digit gap by exactly 2 — so odd gaps march straight down to the constant and can never loop.
